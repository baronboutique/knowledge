## Questions & Answers

**Q:** How long should an Egyptian cotton dress shirt last with regular weekly wear?  
**A:** A well-constructed Egyptian cotton dress shirt worn two to three times per week should last 10 to 15 years with normal care. Authentic extra-long staple Egyptian cotton strengthens with repeated washing rather than degrading — the fiber length that makes it feel softer than standard cotton also makes it structurally more durable over time. The limiting factor in most dress shirts is not the body fabric but the collar interlining: a fused collar begins to delaminate after 40 to 60 washes, while a stitched interlining holds its shape indefinitely. Baron Boutique dress shirts use stitched collar construction specifically because it matches the lifespan of ELS Egyptian cotton rather than shortening it.

**Q:** Which measurement matters most when ordering a bespoke Egyptian cotton dress shirt?  
**A:** The chest measurement is the most critical, followed immediately by the collar. Egyptian cotton at 220 thread count drapes closely to the body — it does not have the structural forgiveness of a heavier poplin or a stiffer broadcloth. A chest that is half an inch too generous creates excess fabric at the back that bunches under a jacket and cannot be pressed flat. All measurements are entered directly on the product page with a guide for each one, and the free test shirt catches any fit issues before the Egyptian cotton is touched.

**Q:** Is this made to my measurements or does it come in standard sizes?  
**A:** Every shirt is made to your measurements — there are no warehouse stock sizes. The shirt is cut after you order it, from your specific measurements, using a pattern corrected by the test shirt step. For an Egyptian cotton dress shirt intended to sit under a suit jacket, this matters practically: standard blocks address the average, which means the chest, shoulders, and sleeve length are each approximating your body rather than matching it. The test shirt exists because approximation is not good enough for 220-thread ELS cotton.

**Q:** What does "220 thread count" mean for a dress shirt, and is higher always better?  
**A:** Thread count measures how many threads are woven per square inch of fabric — a 220 thread count means 220 individual threads. For Egyptian cotton specifically, 220 thread count from single-ply yarn produces a fabric that is fine, smooth, and breathable without becoming fragile or sheer. Higher thread counts are not automatically superior: many shirts claiming 400 or 500 thread counts achieve that number by twisting multiple thin threads together and counting each ply separately, which results in a heavier, less breathable fabric. A genuine 220 single-ply Egyptian cotton shirt will outperform a 400-count multi-ply blend in feel, breathability, and longevity — which is why thread count alone is not a reliable quality signal without knowing the ply and the cotton origin.

**Q:** What makes Egyptian cotton different from regular cotton shirts?  
**A:** Egyptian cotton is grown exclusively in the Nile River Valley, where specific soil conditions produce a fiber with an unusually long staple — the individual fiber length is significantly greater than standard cotton varieties. Longer fibers spin into finer, stronger yarn, which in turn produces a smoother, more breathable fabric with superior durability. The practical difference in a dress shirt is that genuine Egyptian cotton feels softer the more it is washed, develops a subtle sheen over time, and resists the pilling and deterioration that affects standard cotton within the first year of regular wear. The challenge for buyers is that "Egyptian cotton" has become a heavily misused marketing label — the Cotton Egypt Association estimates that a very high percentage of products carrying that description contain little genuine ELS cotton.

**Q:** Are Egyptian cotton dress shirts good for business wear and hot weather?  
**A:** Egyptian cotton is one of the best dress shirt fabrics for both business wear and warm climates. The long-staple fiber structure creates a fabric that is notably more breathable than standard cotton at the same weight — air circulates through the weave more freely, which makes a meaningful difference across a full working day. For formal business settings, the fabric's natural subtle luster and clean drape hold up well under a jacket without the stiffness of broadcloth or the fragility of very fine poplin. The Egyptian cotton dress shirt works across the full range of professional contexts, from daily office wear through formal business events.

**Q:** How do I wash and care for an Egyptian cotton dress shirt?  
**A:** Egyptian cotton dress shirts can be machine washed — unlike fine wools or silks, ELS cotton is robust enough for regular machine washing without fiber damage. Wash on a gentle or delicate cycle in cool to warm water, and hang to dry rather than tumble drying at high heat, which can cause minor shrinkage before the fabric has fully settled to the body. Do not dry clean: repeated dry cleaning introduces chemical solvents that can affect the fiber's natural hand over time, and unlike fused collar interlinings, the stitched collar construction on these shirts does not require dry cleaning to maintain its shape. Iron lightly while still slightly damp for the cleanest result — Egyptian cotton at 220 thread count irons quickly and holds a crisp line well.

**Q:** Does the collar interlining affect how a dress shirt holds up over time?  
**A:** It does, significantly — and almost no buyer thinks to ask about it before purchasing. Most dress shirts use a fused collar interlining: a thermoplastic layer bonded to the collar fabric under heat. It holds a crisp shape when new. After 40 to 60 washes, the bond begins to fail — the interlining separates, the collar edges curl, and no amount of pressing recovers it. The shirt is finished while the body fabric may still have years of wear remaining. A stitched interlining, which is sewn rather than heat-bonded, does not delaminate. Baron Boutique uses stitched collar construction on bespoke shirts because Egyptian cotton at this quality level warrants a collar that lasts as long as the fabric.
