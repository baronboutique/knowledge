Free Test Suit: Confirm the Double Breasted Stance Before We Cut the Wool
The double-breasted suit has one fitting challenge that single-breasted suits do not have. The 6x2 front creates a defined overlap and stance, the position of the two fastening buttons relative to your chest and waist. If the stance is slightly off, the entire front reads as misaligned. You cannot pin-correct a double-breasted stance the way you can adjust a single-breasted jacket at a fitting. It has to be right in the pattern. Before we cut your final suit in Super 120s worsted wool, we make a complete test suit in cotton to your measurements, jacket and trousers, and ship it to you via trackable courier. You wear it.

## Questions & Answers

**Q:** Why does the 6x2 double breasted suit look so different from older double breasted suits?  
**A:** The boxy, unflattering double-breasted suits most people remember came from a combination of heavy shoulder padding, fused construction, and a high button stance that created a wide, rigid chest. The modern 6x2 has none of those features. The button stance is lower, creating a longer V-line. The construction is canvas front rather than fused, so the jacket drapes and moves rather than holding a fixed shape. The shoulders are natural rather than padded. The waist is suppressed. The result is a suit that elongates rather than widens, and that reads as intentional and contemporary rather than dated.

**Q:** What does 6x2 mean on a double breasted suit?  
**A:** 6x2 means six buttons arranged in two columns, with only two of them functional fastening buttons. The other four are decorative and define the visual symmetry of the front. The two fastening buttons are typically the middle pair, and the suit is designed to be worn closed at those two points. The 6x2 is the most common modern double-breasted configuration and the one that works best for most body types. A 6x1 configuration fastens at only one point and creates a different, slightly more casual silhouette.

**Q:** What is a canvas front and why does it matter in a double breasted suit?  
**A:** A canvas front is a layer of woven horsehair canvas hand-stitched into the chest of the jacket, between the outer fabric and the lining. It floats rather than being glued in place. Over the first months of wearing, it molds to your specific chest shape, making the jacket feel increasingly like your own body rather than a rigid structure you are wearing. In a fused jacket, the interlining is glued and holds a fixed shape regardless of your body. Fused construction often causes bubbling and separation over time, especially through dry cleaning. In a double-breasted suit, the canvas front matters more than in a single-breasted suit because the 6x2 front creates a large, unbroken surface that shows every imperfection in construction.

**Q:** Does a double breasted suit work for my body type?  
**A:** When built correctly, the 6x2 double-breasted suit works for a wide range of body types. The overlapping front adds visual width to the chest, making slimmer frames look more substantial. The defined peak lapels and button stance draw the eye inward and down, creating the appearance of a narrower waist. For taller men, the suit creates a proportionate silhouette. For shorter men, the leg-lengthening trouser cut and lower button stance help elongate the frame. The key is correct fit, which is why the free test suit process matters particularly for the double-breasted silhouette.
