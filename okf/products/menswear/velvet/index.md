Cheap Velvet Looks Like a Costume. The Difference Is the Cloth and the Fit.
Velvet is the one fabric that changes the room. The cut pile absorbs direct light and throws back the warm, low light of an evening event, so a burgundy or midnight jacket reads deeper and richer than anything flat-woven beside it. It is the fabric of the holiday party, the winter wedding, and black tie with a point of view. It is also the fabric most often ruined by a bad version, because the same pile that catches light also catches every pulling seam and every dropped shoulder. A stiff, off-the-rack velvet jacket in a boxy S, M, or L is what turns velvet into a costume.

## Questions & Answers

**Q:** When can you wear a velvet jacket?  
**A:** A velvet jacket belongs to the cooler half of the year and to the evening. It is at its best from autumn through winter and into early spring, at holiday parties, Christmas and New Year&#8217;s dinners, winter weddings, cocktail events, and black-tie or black-tie-optional evenings. The fabric reads as festive and luxurious under warm, low light, which is exactly the setting most of these occasions provide. It is not a daytime or high-summer garment.

**Q:** Is a velvet jacket appropriate for black tie?  
**A:** Yes. A velvet dinner jacket or velvet smoking jacket is a recognized black-tie option, worn with black formal trousers, a white or black dress shirt, and a bow tie. Velvet is, in fact, one of the traditional evening fabrics, and a burgundy, midnight, or black velvet jacket is a confident alternative to a plain black tuxedo jacket at any black-tie-optional event. For strict black tie, keep the trousers and accessories formal and let the velvet be the single point of interest.

**Q:** Can I wear a velvet jacket to a wedding?  
**A:** Yes, and it photographs beautifully doing it. Velvet&#8217;s pile creates depth in images that flat fabrics cannot match, which is why grooms and wedding guests choose it for autumn and winter weddings. For a summer or daytime wedding velvet can run warm and read heavy, so it suits an evening reception better than an afternoon ceremony in the sun. The velvet groomsmen blazer in this collection is built specifically for a wedding party that wants texture in the photographs.

**Q:** What color velvet is the most versatile?  
**A:** Burgundy is the most-requested and arguably the most versatile, festive enough for the holidays yet restrained enough to wear to any evening event. Midnight blue and navy are close behind and read richer than black under venue light. Black velvet is the quiet, most formal choice. Green is a striking, characterful option that works well at events with a festive or creative dress code. Most of these pieces are available in up to 11 velvet colors at the same price, so the choice is yours.

**Q:** What is the difference between a velvet dinner jacket and a velvet smoking jacket?  
**A:** A velvet dinner jacket is cut like a formal jacket with structured shoulders and satin lapels, made to be worn out to black-tie events with formal trousers. A velvet smoking jacket is softer and more relaxed, traditionally a garment of private leisure, and is signalled by its shawl collar and braided frogging closure rather than notch or peak lapels. In practice both are worn to evening occasions today, but the smoking jacket carries a more historic, at-home character while the dinner jacket is the sharper formal statement.

**Q:** Does a velvet jacket look like a costume?  
**A:** Cheap velvet does, and for two reasons: the cloth is thin and shiny, and the fit is wrong. A boxy off-the-rack velvet jacket in a generic size, cut from low-grade fabric, is what gives velvet its fancy-dress reputation. A velvet piece in a quality poly-cotton velvet that holds its pile, cut to your actual measurements on a canvas front, reads as tailoring instead. Fit is the deciding factor, which is why every piece here is made to your measurements and confirmed with a free test garment before the velvet is cut.

**Q:** What do I wear with a velvet jacket?  
**A:** For a formal look, wear black tailored or tuxedo trousers, a crisp dress shirt in white or black, and a bow tie; the plain black grounds the velvet and lets it be the statement. For a dressed-down look, a velvet jacket works over dark slim jeans and a fine-gauge knit or patterned shirt for a cocktail evening or a smart dinner. Keep accessories simple in either case, because velvet already carries the outfit and does not need competition.

**Q:** Can I dress a velvet jacket down for a less formal event?  
**A:** Yes. One of velvet&#8217;s advantages is its range. The same jacket that reads black-tie over formal trousers reads relaxed and modern over dark jeans with a patterned shirt and no tie. A single-button velvet dinner jacket or the groomsmen blazer both handle this shift well. It is one of the few pieces that can carry you from a formal dinner to a stylish night out with only a change of trousers and shirt.

**Q:** How do I care for a velvet jacket?  
**A:** Velvet is dry-clean only. Between wears, hang it on a broad shaped hanger so the shoulders keep their line, and use a soft garment brush in the direction of the pile to lift dust and keep the nap even. To release light creases, steam it gently by hanging it near a hot shower or holding a garment steamer a few inches away; never press velvet with a hot iron, as direct heat and pressure crush the pile permanently. Stored and brushed correctly, a good velvet jacket keeps its depth for years.
