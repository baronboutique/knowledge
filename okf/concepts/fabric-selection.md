# How We Choose Fabric

Customers often begin by choosing a fabric with their eyes. We begin by understanding how they intend to live with the garment.

A recent example: a customer wanted a heavy tweed jacket because they loved its rich texture and heritage look. During the conversation we learned the jacket would be worn primarily in Dubai. Tweed would certainly have achieved the appearance they admired, but it would have spent most of its life hanging in the wardrobe because of the climate. Rather than simply accepting the request, we explained the trade-off. The customer wasn't really asking for tweed; they were asking for a jacket with character. Once we understood that, we recommended a lighter wool with a textured weave that offered similar visual depth while being far more comfortable in warm weather. The final garment looked remarkably close to what the customer originally imagined, and it became something they could actually wear.

That is how we approach every fabric recommendation. We don't begin with fabric. We begin with the customer's objective.

## The fabrics we work with

Our workshop regularly works with worsted wool, tropical wool, flannel, tweed, boiled wool, cashmere, cotton, linen, Himalayan hemp, Dupioni silk, satin silk, crepe silk and other raw silks, velvet, and a variety of natural fiber blends. The honest answer to "what do you work with?" is almost everything that can be tailored well.

Choosing between them is never a matter of selecting the "best" fabric. The same customer could receive three completely different recommendations depending on where they live, how often they intend to wear the garment, and what experience they expect from it. There are fabrics we sometimes discourage, not because they are poor fabrics, but because they are poor choices for a particular project. A fabric can be beautiful and still be the wrong answer.

If we had to choose the fabric we enjoy tailoring most, it would be wool. Not because it is the most luxurious, but because wool works with the tailor: it responds beautifully to steam, shaping, and pressing, and lets us sculpt the garment rather than simply sew it together. Few fabrics are as forgiving or as rewarding during construction (see [Understanding Wool](wool.md)).

## How we think about fabric weight

Customers often ask, "What fabric weight should I choose?" Our answer is usually another question: "Where will you be wearing it?"

A 320-gram wool may be perfect for someone in Edinburgh and completely impractical for someone spending most of the year in Dubai. Likewise, a customer may assume the lightest cloth is always the coolest. In reality, comfort depends on far more than weight alone: fiber type, weave, garment construction, and how the customer actually wears the garment all influence the result.

We don't recommend a weight because it sits within a particular category. We recommend it because we understand the customer's climate, lifestyle, and expectations. The best fabric is not the one with the most impressive specification. It is the one the customer reaches for again and again.
