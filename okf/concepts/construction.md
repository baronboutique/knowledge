# Construction

Once the fit is signed off, the corrected pattern is cut in the final fabric and the garment is built. Customers sometimes ask whether everything is sewn by hand, expecting the answer to be yes. Our honest answer is better than yes: we use hand work where hand work produces the superior garment, and machine work where a properly executed machine stitch gives superior consistency and durability. The goal is never to maximize hand sewing for its own sake. It is to use the right technique in the right place, and knowing which is which is precisely what decades in the workshop teach.

## Canvas: how we choose the structure

When a customer asks why one jacket drapes beautifully for decades and another bubbles after a few years of dry cleaning, the answer is usually hidden inside the chest. The internal canvas is the skeleton of a tailored garment, and we choose it per project rather than applying one method to everything:

- **Full canvas** is our recommendation for bespoke suits, overcoats, and jackets whenever the fabric and design allow. The canvas runs from the shoulders through the front panels to the hem, and the garment gradually molds to its wearer over years of use. For a garment meant to be kept, this is the construction we believe in.
- **Half canvas** enters the conversation for lighter jackets, or when the customer wants extra softness or reduced weight. Less structure is sometimes the right answer, and we say so.
- **Fused construction we avoid** wherever longevity, drape, and natural movement are the priorities, because adhesives age. Heat, moisture, and repeated pressing can cause bubbling or delamination that cannot truly be repaired. A fused garment often looks sharp on the hanger and new; the difference appears with years.

Our canvases are traditionally woven from natural fibers: horsehair, wool, cotton, and linen. Weight is matched to the cloth, because a heavy tweed coat needs a different skeleton than a lightweight tropical wool jacket.

Why insist on hand canvassing? A hand-canvassed garment is attached with many carefully placed stitches instead of adhesive, so the outer fabric and the canvas move independently while staying coordinated. Over time that produces a chest that develops natural shape, a rolling lapel, better drape, greater comfort, easier alterations decades later, and a longer life. Every one of those stitches is slower than glue; each one contributes to how the garment behaves rather than how it looks on day one.

## The build order

Every garment presents its own challenges, but the workshop's sequence is deliberate, and the order itself is a decision: structure before beauty.

1. **Pattern development.** Measurements, posture, balance, shoulder angle, and design details are settled before any fabric is cut (see [Pattern Drafting](pattern-drafting.md)).
2. **Fabric preparation.** The cloth is inspected, and pattern pieces are laid to respect grain direction, pattern matching, nap (for velvet or corduroy), and stripe or check alignment. Cutting happens only after this planning, because a matching error survives forever.
3. **Internal construction.** The invisible structure is built first: canvas, chest pieces, shoulder reinforcement, internal support. This stage determines how the coat behaves for years, which is why it comes before anything the customer will ever see.
4. **Assembly.** Fronts, backs, sleeves, collars, facings, and pockets come together. Pocket placement, symmetry, balance, and alignment are checked repeatedly, because this is where precision is won or lost.
5. **Hand tailoring.** Canvas attachment, collar shaping, lapel roll, sleeve setting, buttonholes, finishing details: the stage where the garment gains its character.
6. **Fittings where possible** before completion (see [Fittings](fittings.md)).
7. **Final finishing.** Lining completed, buttons attached, threads removed, edges refined, final pressing, inspection.

## Where the hand work goes

Hand work at Baron Boutique goes well beyond buttonholes. Depending on the garment, our tailors hand-work collar attachment, under-collar felting, canvas padding, lapel shaping, sleeve insertion, lining attachment, blind hemming, edge finishing, pick stitching, bar tacks, reinforcement stitching, and final finishing. Armholes are hand-finished because hand work gives greater control during sleeve setting and a cleaner, more comfortable fit; every buttonhole is sewn by hand, front and cuffs alike.

What tells a collector a garment was made this way? Rarely anything loud. A naturally rolling lapel rather than a pressed crease. A collar that hugs the neck. Sleeves that rotate with the arm. Balanced pattern matching, subtle pick stitching, a soft chest instead of a rigid front, drape without tension or distortion. The greatest compliment a handmade garment receives is that nothing calls attention to itself; everything simply feels right.

## Materials: the unseen choices

A bespoke garment is only as good as the materials hidden inside it, so the unseen components get the same deliberation as the outer cloth:

- **Linings** are chosen by climate, breathability, durability, and intended use: Bemberg cupro, acetate, viscose, silk, cotton, or a premium polyester when durability or the customer's preference calls for it. The question is never "which lining is best" but "which lining suits this garment's life."
- **Canvas and interlinings** are natural-fiber, matched to the weight and behavior of the outer fabric.
- **Thread** is primarily premium polyester for its strength, longevity, and color stability, with specialty threads selected for individual hand operations.
- **Buttons** are the customer's choice: genuine horn, corozo (vegetable ivory), mother-of-pearl, metal, wood, premium resin, or fabric-covered. Historical reproductions often require custom button sourcing, because on a replica the wrong button is the first thing an enthusiast notices.
- **Fabrics** come from mills worldwide, alongside specialty textiles customers supply under the CMT model (see [How We Choose Fabric](fabric-selection.md) and [The Bespoke Commission Process](bespoke-commission-process.md)). The cashmere collection uses 100% Grade A cashmere sustainably sourced from the Tibetan Plateau and handcrafted in Nepal.

## Time and hands

No single tailor makes an entire garment; traditional tailoring is collaborative by design, because pattern making, cutting, canvas preparation, assembly, pressing, hand finishing, and quality inspection are each their own craft. Depending on complexity, roughly 4 to 6 specialists contribute to one garment.

A bespoke coat typically takes several weeks from cutting to completion. The total skilled labor involved often exceeds dozens of hours across multiple specialist tailors; the exact time depends on the garment's complexity, construction method, and level of hand finishing. We would rather let each stage take the time it needs than rush the one garment a customer may keep for decades.

## Finishing and dispatch

The final stage is not packing a box; it is the last chance to catch what everyone else missed. Each completed garment goes through comprehensive thread trimming, button security checks, seam inspection, measurement verification, pressing and shaping, and a final visual inspection under proper lighting. Only then is it prepared for travel: breathable garment bags where appropriate, tissue wrapping, moisture protection, reinforced export packaging built for international shipping. Many of our garments travel thousands of miles to their owners; our responsibility is that they arrive ready to be worn, not just ready to be unboxed. The finished garment ships by courier with tracking.
