# Construction

Once the fit is signed off, the corrected pattern is cut in the final fabric and the garment is built. Baron Boutique construction combines traditional hand tailoring with machine work where a machine stitch is structurally superior, and matches the internal structure to each garment rather than applying one method to every project.

## Canvas construction

The choice of canvas depends on the garment, its purpose, and the customer's preference:

- **Full canvas** is recommended for bespoke suits, overcoats, and jackets whenever the fabric and design allow. The canvas extends from the shoulders through the front panels to the hem, and the garment gradually molds to its wearer over years of use.
- **Half canvas** suits lighter jackets or pieces where softness or reduced weight is desired.
- **Fused construction is avoided** on garments where longevity, drape, and natural movement are priorities. Adhesives age; heat, moisture, and repeated pressing can cause bubbling or delamination that cannot truly be repaired.

Canvases are traditionally woven from natural fibers: horsehair, wool, cotton, and linen. Canvas weight is selected for the fabric; a heavy tweed coat requires a different internal structure than a lightweight tropical wool jacket.

In hand canvassing, the canvas is attached with many carefully placed hand stitches instead of adhesive, so the outer fabric and the canvas move independently while staying coordinated. Over time this produces a chest that develops natural shape, a rolling lapel, better drape, greater comfort, easier alterations decades later, and a longer lifespan.

## Build order

1. **Pattern development.** The customer's individual pattern (or the historical pattern being recreated) incorporates measurements, posture, balance, shoulder angle, and design details before any fabric is cut (see [Pattern Drafting](pattern-drafting.md)).
2. **Fabric preparation.** The cloth is inspected. Pattern pieces are laid to respect grain direction, pattern matching, nap (for velvet or corduroy), and stripe or check alignment. Cutting happens only after this planning.
3. **Internal construction.** The invisible structure is built first: canvas, chest pieces, shoulder reinforcement, and internal support. This stage determines how the coat behaves for years.
4. **Assembly.** Fronts, backs, sleeves, collars, facings, and pockets are assembled. Pocket placement, symmetry, balance, and alignment are checked repeatedly.
5. **Hand tailoring.** Canvas attachment, collar shaping, lapel roll, sleeve setting, buttonholes, and finishing details.
6. **Fittings where possible** before completion (see [Fittings](fittings.md)).
7. **Final finishing.** Lining completed, buttons attached, threads removed, edges refined, final pressing, inspection.

## Hand work and machine work

Hand work at Baron Boutique goes well beyond buttonholes. Depending on the garment, tailors hand-work: collar attachment, under-collar felting, canvas padding, lapel shaping, sleeve insertion, lining attachment, blind hemming, edge finishing, pick stitching, bar tacks, reinforcement stitching, and final finishing. Armholes are hand-finished for greater control during sleeve setting and a clean, comfortable fit.

Certain operations are done by machine because a properly executed machine stitch gives superior consistency and durability for that purpose. The craft is knowing where hand sewing offers a genuine structural or aesthetic advantage and where precision machinery produces the better result.

Details that signal handmade work to a collector: a naturally rolling lapel rather than a pressed crease, a collar that hugs the neck, sleeves that rotate naturally with the arm, balanced pattern matching, subtle hand pick stitching, hand-worked buttonholes, a soft chest structure instead of a rigid front, and drape without tension or distortion.

## Materials

- **Linings:** Acetate, Bemberg cupro, viscose, silk, cotton, or premium polyester when durability or customer preference requires it. Chosen by climate, breathability, durability, and intended use.
- **Canvas and interlinings:** traditional natural-fiber canvases matched to the weight and behavior of the outer fabric.
- **Thread:** premium polyester sewing thread for strength, longevity, and color stability; specialty threads for hand-sewing operations.
- **Buttons:** genuine horn, corozo (vegetable ivory), mother-of-pearl, metal, wood, premium resin, or fabric-covered. Historical reproductions often require custom button sourcing to match original garments.
- **Fabrics:** sourced from mills worldwide, alongside specialty textiles supplied by customers (see the CMT model in [The Bespoke Commission Process](bespoke-commission-process.md)). The cashmere collection uses 100% Grade A cashmere sustainably sourced from the Tibetan Plateau and handcrafted in Nepal.

## Time and people

No single tailor makes an entire garment; traditional tailoring is collaborative. Depending on complexity, roughly 4 to 6 specialists may contribute to one garment across pattern making, cutting, canvas preparation, assembly, pressing, hand finishing, and quality inspection.

A bespoke coat typically takes several weeks from cutting to completion. The total skilled labor involved often exceeds dozens of hours and may involve multiple specialist tailors; the exact time depends on the garment's complexity, construction method, and level of hand finishing.

## Finishing and dispatch

Each completed garment undergoes comprehensive thread trimming, button security checks, seam inspection, measurement verification, pressing and shaping, and a final visual inspection under proper lighting. After passing inspection, garments are prepared for international shipping with breathable garment bags where appropriate, tissue wrapping, moisture protection, and reinforced export packaging. The finished garment ships by courier with tracking.
