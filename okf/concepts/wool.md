# Understanding Wool

Customers often think of wool as a single fabric. From a tailor's perspective, it is an entire family of fabrics, each solving a different problem. We don't categorize wool by technical definitions; we categorize it by the problems it solves.

## Which wool for which problem

- **Worsted wool.** When someone wants a business suit that will spend five days a week in the office, we often begin with worsted, because it maintains a clean, professional appearance through exactly that kind of life.
- **Tropical wool.** When someone lives in a warmer climate but still wants the look of a classic business suit, tropical wool is usually part of the conversation, because it delivers much of the same elegance while remaining noticeably more comfortable.
- **Flannel.** When a customer asks for a winter jacket that feels relaxed rather than formal, flannel often becomes the recommendation.
- **Tweed.** When they want texture, heritage character, and something that becomes more beautiful with age, we begin discussing tweed. Climate matters here: for a customer in a hot climate who loves the tweed look, we may steer toward a lighter textured wool that gives similar visual depth without the weight (see [How We Choose Fabric](fabric-selection.md)).
- **Boiled wool.** When a customer is recreating a historical garment or needs a coat with body and warmth, boiled wool frequently enters the conversation. It has also served screen-replica work, where the original costume's structure demands a cloth with real presence.

## Why wool is the fabric we most enjoy cutting

Wool works with the tailor. It responds beautifully to steam, shaping, and pressing, which lets us sculpt a garment rather than simply sew it together. That behavior matters most in canvassed construction, where the cloth and the internal structure must learn the wearer's shape together over years (see [Construction](construction.md)). Few fabrics are as forgiving during construction, and few reward hand work as visibly.
