# Fittings

A set of photos arrives: the customer standing in a light blue shell, front, sides, back, sometimes held next to a movie still. To the customer it is a progress update. To the workshop it is the fitting, the moment 26 years of looking at bodies in unfinished garments earns its keep. Baron Boutique fits remotely, so everything a traditional tailor would do with hands and pins, we do by knowing exactly what to look for in a photograph.

## How we read the photos

The review follows a fixed order, learned by repetition: look at the center of the body first, glance upward from the center, then downward from it. The first checks are chest, waist, hips, and shoulder width, because if those are correct or near correct, everything else is easily fixable; if they are wrong, nothing else matters yet.

Then come the subtle reads, the things customers cannot see on themselves: whether the left and right sides sit low-high, how the arm hangs, whether the neck leans slightly forward in a way that creates pulls elsewhere in the garment. Posture and stance never appear in a measurement list, but they appear in every photo. A photograph often reveals what neither measurements nor the customer can describe, which is why the toile stage exists at all.

## Translating feedback into corrections

Customers describe what they want in plain words, not pattern terms, and we treat their words as the goal, not the instruction. When a customer says "make the front 3.5 inches longer," our first step is not adding 3.5 inches. We verify the statement against the photos and pivot points, the natural waist, where the panels currently finish, look back at the reference image, and then decide the actual correction (see [Trial Garment / Toile](trial-garment-toile.md) and [Pattern Drafting](pattern-drafting.md)). Experience says the stated number and the needed change often differ, and the garment must obey the need, not the number.

No adjustment is made in isolation. Every correction influences the balance, proportion, and hang of the whole garment, so the surrounding pattern pieces are reviewed and refined together with the requested change.

## When the customer wants something we would not choose

Every bespoke garment is a collaboration. When a customer requests something we believe will hurt the fit, balance, or appearance, we explain our concern and offer our recommendation before proceeding. Often a conversation is enough, and sometimes we persuade them toward a better path. But it is the customer's garment, and their preferences matter; our responsibility is that they understand the likely outcome before deciding. We never make design changes without discussing them first. Clear communication is as much a part of tailoring as precise stitching.

## Sign-off

The fit is final only when two judgments agree. From the customer, the words we most want to hear: "I'm happy with the overall fit." From us, a last review of the fitting photos to confirm the garment hangs correctly and no structural issue remains that the customer may not notice. Only when customer confidence and workshop assessment point to the same conclusion do we cut the actual fabric (see [Construction](construction.md)). A bespoke garment never moves to its final stage on assumption alone.
