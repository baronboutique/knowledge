# Velvet

Velvet is one of the few fabrics that changes as you move around it. Its nap catches light differently from every angle, which is exactly why it creates such dramatic garments, and why so many of our frock coats, dinner jackets, and screen-inspired pieces are cut from it.

## A velvet story

One of our most rewarding velvet commissions involved a customer who wanted a screen-accurate frock coat inspired by a television costume. Like many customers, they arrived focused almost entirely on the color: they had spent weeks comparing photographs online and wanted the velvet to match the screen as closely as possible.

Color, however, was only part of the conversation. Two velvets with the same color can behave completely differently once they are made into a coat. One may drape beautifully but wrinkle easily; another may hold its shape better while reflecting light differently because of the nap. After discussing how the coat would be worn, we recommended a cotton velvet that offered the right balance of structure, appearance, and durability for the design. It achieved the rich character the customer wanted while providing enough body for the long frock coat to hang properly.

The most important decision wasn't choosing the color. It was choosing the velvet that would continue looking right long after the first wearing. That is how we approach every velvet commission.

## Which velvet?

Customers sometimes ask, "What's the best velvet?" There isn't one. The right velvet depends entirely on the garment.

For many of our frock coats, movie replicas, and structured jackets, we typically recommend cotton velvet or poly-cotton velvet, because these provide the right balance of weight, structure, durability, and workability. Long coats and tailored garments need a fabric that supports the silhouette rather than collapsing under its own weight, and cotton and poly-cotton velvet let us achieve that balance while remaining practical to tailor.

For evening jackets, the decision may depend on how formal the customer wants the finished garment to appear: some prefer the softer appearance of other velvet types, while others value durability and easier maintenance. For gowns, drape often becomes the deciding factor, so the recommendation may differ again.

We don't begin by asking, "Which velvet would you like?" We begin by asking, "What do you want the garment to become?" Once we understand that, the appropriate velvet usually becomes clear.

## Working with velvet

Velvet asks for patience. Unlike many other fabrics, it rarely forgives carelessness.

Before a single piece is cut, every pattern piece must be planned so the nap runs consistently throughout the garment. If one panel is accidentally cut in the opposite direction, it may appear to be an entirely different shade even though it came from the same roll. Cutting also requires greater attention because velvet tends to shift as it is handled.

Pressing requires restraint. Unlike wool, velvet should never be treated as a fabric that can simply be flattened into shape: excessive heat or pressure can permanently crush the pile and leave marks that cannot be removed. Throughout construction, we allow the fabric to behave naturally instead of forcing it into submission.

One of the most common mistakes less experienced workshops make is treating velvet like ordinary suiting fabric, relying on excessive pressing to solve construction issues rather than careful planning from the beginning. Good velvet tailoring isn't about correcting mistakes afterward. It's about avoiding them before they happen.

## Who should not choose velvet?

Sometimes the best velvet commission is the one we never make. Customers occasionally choose velvet because they have fallen in love with a photograph without considering how they plan to wear the garment afterward.

One of the first questions we ask is, "How often do you expect to wear this?" If the answer is once or twice a year for special occasions, velvet is often an excellent choice. If the customer wants a jacket for every week at the office or frequent travel, we usually recommend exploring textured wool instead: many woven wools offer depth, richness, and character while being easier to maintain and more versatile for everyday use (see [Understanding Wool](wool.md)).

We never discourage velvet because it is difficult to tailor. We simply believe every fabric should suit the life the customer intends to give it.

## Caring for velvet

The best care begins before the garment is ever worn. A velvet coat should always be stored on a properly shaped hanger that supports the shoulders and lets the garment hang freely; crushing it in a crowded closet for extended periods can affect the pile. When wrinkles develop, steaming is preferable to pressing: steam relaxes the fibers without flattening the surface that gives velvet its distinctive appearance. Never place a hot iron directly onto the face of the fabric; excessive heat and pressure can permanently damage the pile.

Perhaps the most important advice we give is this: velvet is meant to develop character through careful wear. The subtle changes that appear over time are not flaws; they are part of what makes every velvet garment uniquely its own. A well-made velvet coat shouldn't spend its life protected in a garment bag. It should spend its life being enjoyed.
