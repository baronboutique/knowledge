# Trial Garment (Toile)

The trial garment (test garment, or toile) is a fit shell made from the customer's individual pattern and shipped to the customer before the final garment is cut. Its job is fit, not fabric: it can be made and shipped before the final fabric is even sourced.

## What it is made from

Toiles are always cut in light-colored fabric so that dark markings and stitch markings are clearly visible on photos. The typical toile is a lightweight light blue poly-cotton blend shell. The toile fabric follows the final garment's weight class: a heavier cotton is used for heavy outerwear so the toile more closely reflects how the finished garment will behave, and similar-weight satins are used for gowns so the fitting represents the final drape. The fabric is chosen for the purpose of the fitting; the objective is always the most accurate information before the customer's final fabric is cut.

## One toile, then the final garment

The standard commission is one toile followed by the final garment, and the price includes the test garment, the final garment, and both shipments. Two exceptions:

- A customer unsure about the fit can request a second toile at additional cost.
- Baron Boutique itself recommends a second toile for exceptionally valuable garments, or when the complexity, uniqueness, or cost of the final fabric justifies another round of verification.

The goal is never more fitting garments than necessary: the correct result in the fewest steps, with both the customer and the workshop fully confident before the final fabric is cut.

Repeat orders skip the toile entirely when only the color or a slight style detail changes and the measurements are identical to the previous, already verified order.

## Applying corrections without a second test

Corrections from the toile review usually go straight into the final garment. The confidence to do that is experience-based: a customer's statement does not always translate to the exact measurement they mention, so Baron Boutique works from pivot points instead of taking the number at face value. In a tailcoat commission, that meant locating the customer's natural waist in the toile photos, checking how far below the natural waist the front panel ended, referring back to the customer's reference image, then putting everything together, discussing, and deciding the actual pattern change (see [Fittings](fittings.md) and [Pattern Drafting](pattern-drafting.md)).
