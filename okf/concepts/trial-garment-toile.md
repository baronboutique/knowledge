# Trial Garment (Toile)

Customers sometimes ask why we ship a plain test garment before making the real one; a few even ask if they can skip it to save time. Our answer comes from experience: the test garment is the single cheapest insurance in bespoke tailoring. It is a fit shell made from the customer's individual pattern, and its entire job is to let a wrong assumption fail in inexpensive cloth instead of in the customer's fabric. That is why it can ship before the final fabric is even sourced: its job is fit, not fabric.

## What it is made from, and why

Toiles are always cut in light-colored fabric, because alteration lines, balance marks, and stitch markings must be clearly visible in the photos the customer sends back. The typical toile is a lightweight light blue poly-cotton shell. But the toile fabric follows the final garment's weight class, because a fit test only means something if the test cloth behaves like the real one: we use a heavier cotton for heavy outerwear so the toile reflects how the finished garment will hang, and similar-weight satins for gowns so the fitting represents the final drape. The fabric is chosen for the purpose of the fitting; the objective is always the most accurate information before the customer's final fabric is cut.

## One toile, then the final garment

Our standard process is one test garment followed by the final garment, and the price includes the test garment, the final garment, and both shipments. Experience shows one well-evaluated toile gives us the information we need. Two exceptions, both deliberate:

- A customer unsure about the fit can request a second toile at additional cost, because their confidence matters as much as ours.
- We ourselves recommend a second toile for exceptionally valuable garments, or when the complexity, uniqueness, or cost of the final fabric justifies another round of verification. When a mistake would be expensive enough, a second test stops being a luxury.

Repeat orders skip the toile entirely when only the color or a slight style detail changes and the measurements match a previously verified order, because that pattern has already proven itself on the customer's body.

Our goal is never to produce more fitting garments than necessary. It is the correct result in the fewest steps, with both the customer and the workshop fully confident before the final fabric is cut.

## Applying corrections without a second test

Corrections from the toile review usually go straight into the final garment, and customers sometimes ask how we dare. The confidence is experience-based, and it rests on one habit: we never take the customer's stated number at face value. When someone says "make the front 3.5 inches longer," our first step is not adding 3.5 inches to the pattern. We study the photos, locate pivot points such as the natural waist, check where the panels currently finish on the body, compare against the customer's reference image, and only then decide what the pattern change actually is (see [Fittings](fittings.md) and [Pattern Drafting](pattern-drafting.md)). The customer tells us what they want to see; the toile photos tell us what to change.
