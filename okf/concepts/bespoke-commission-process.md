# The Bespoke Commission Process

Customers rarely arrive at Baron Boutique asking for "a bespoke commission." They arrive with an idea: a screenshot of a coat from a film, a wedding date, a suit they saw once and never forgot. Our first job is not to take the order. It is to understand what the customer is actually trying to achieve, because every decision that follows, fabric, pattern, proportions, fittings, flows from that answer.

Baron Boutique is an independent tailoring house founded in Kathmandu, Nepal in 2000. Every garment is made after an order is confirmed. We do not work from pre-made blocks or standard sizes: every order is drafted from scratch around the customer's own design references and measurements. This document walks through the journey as it actually happens, using a screen-inspired tailcoat commission (the [1989 Joker tailcoat](https://baronboutique.com/1989-joker-tailcoat/)) as the working example.

## The stages, and why each exists

1. **Inquiry.** The customer reaches out through any channel (website, email, Instagram) with reference pictures, screenshots, sketches, or text details. The more references, the better we can read the design's intent.
2. **Design analysis.** We study the references before quoting, because the honest answer to "can you make this?" depends on understanding what "this" really is. We confirm feasibility and give an initial price.
3. **Measurements.** The customer supplies the measurement set for their garment type, starting with a fit preference and height; the exact set differs for men's and women's garments and per item (see [Measurement](measurement.md) for the full sets). When a customer cannot be measured, we can begin from height, weight, and chest size alone, because experience lets us derive the rest.
4. **Fabric decision.** We begin with the customer's objective, not the fabric (see [How We Choose Fabric](fabric-selection.md)). We source fabric when possible. When exact color is the customer's top priority, we recommend they source the fabric themselves: colors render differently on different monitors, and what we judge as the correct shade may not match their expectation. When weight or performance matters more than a precise shade, we source it. Customer-supplied fabric follows the CMT (Cut, Make and Trim) model, and we advise the required yardage from their measurements.
5. **Order confirmation.** Invoice is sent (PayPal for international clients). The commission price includes the test garment, the final garment, and both shipments, because we consider the test garment part of making the garment, not an extra.
6. **Pattern development.** A new individual pattern is drafted from scratch (see [Pattern Drafting](pattern-drafting.md)). Where the reference picture and the customer's proportions conflict, we ask the customer to choose between "look" (match the picture) and "accuracy" (proportional to their own body). We ask rather than assume, because only the customer knows which matters more to them.
7. **Test garment.** A trial shell in marking-visible toile fabric ships to the customer (see [Trial Garment / Toile](trial-garment-toile.md)). It can ship before the final fabric is even sourced, because its job is fit, not fabric.
8. **Remote fit review.** The customer wears the test garment and sends photos, often side by side with their references. We read fit from those photos using a practiced center-first method (see [Fittings](fittings.md)), and the customer describes desired changes in plain words.
9. **Pattern correction.** We translate the customer's words into pattern changes rather than applying their numbers blindly. In the tailcoat example, "lapel points toward the chest" and "front panels 3.5 inches longer with a more downward angle" became specific redrafted lines, checked against the whole garment's balance.
10. **Final construction.** The corrected pattern is cut in the final fabric and built with extensive hand work: buttonholes, armhole stitching, and canvassing are all done by hand where hand work earns its place (see [Construction](construction.md)).
11. **Delivery and follow-up.** The finished garment ships by courier with tracking, and we follow up after delivery, because the garment's life begins when it arrives, not when it leaves.

## What experience has taught us to do differently

- **No pre-made blocks.** Each commission gets its own pattern because bodies are not standard, and neither are the designs our customers bring. A first-of-its-kind design takes roughly 6 to 8 hours of drafting; a design similar to previous work takes a few hours.
- **The "look or accuracy" question.** The person in a reference photo and the customer almost never share a body. We learned to surface this conflict early and let the customer decide, because discovering it at the fitting stage is too late. The choice shapes lapel width, tail length, cutaway angle, and every proportional element.
- **Test garment included by default.** Every commission includes a test garment and two shipments. The only exception is a repeat order where nothing has changed but color or a slight style detail and the measurements are identical to a previously verified order, because there the pattern has already earned our confidence.
- **Remote-first fitting.** Most clients never visit in person. The entire fit process works through shipped test garments and photo review, refined over 26 years of comparing customer measurements against our archive of completed commissions.
- **Screen and replica work.** For movie-inspired and television-inspired garments, the pattern is developed from screen references supplied by the customer. These are replicas and screen-inspired garments, not licensed merchandise, and we say so plainly.
