# Pattern Drafting

A customer sends us screenshots of a coat from a film and a list of measurements, and asks a question that sounds simple: "Can you make this fit me?" Everything in our pattern room exists to answer it honestly. Baron Boutique does not work from pre-made blocks; every order is drafted from scratch around the customer's design references and measurements, because the question is never "how do we adapt a standard coat," it is "how do we build this design around this body."

## From reference to pattern

1. **We analyze everything the customer sends.** Text details, sketches, screenshots, photos. Before drafting, we need to understand the character of the garment, not just its outline.
2. **We sketch the parts, not the whole.** The first drafting step is a simple pencil sketch on paper, deliberately out of sequence: the tail shape in one corner of the page, the lapel shape in another. These are working sketches, not drawings. Sketching the parts individually is how we get the feel of a design before committing to a full draft, because a garment that looks unified on screen is really a set of decisions that must each be understood.
3. **We draft.** A first-of-its-kind design takes roughly 6 to 8 hours of analysis and drafting. A design similar to previous Baron Boutique work takes a few hours, because earlier commissions have already taught us its problems.

## Deciding proportions from two ends

Lapel width, collar shape, sleeve design, tail length, front cutaway angle: none of these are read off a chart. We work from two directions at once and let them argue.

- **What the picture shows.** The customer's references set the target look.
- **What the measurements say.** The customer's body sets the proportions. Experience supplies the rules of thumb: a standard peak lapel on a 40-inch chest usually sits around 3 to 3.5 inches wide, because that keeps it visually balanced; on a 52-inch chest the same lapel grows to 4 to 4.5 inches for exactly the same reason. The larger the wearer, the larger the details must be to look proportionate.

## The "look or accuracy" decision

The person in the reference photo and the customer almost never have the same body, so sometimes the two ends cannot be reconciled. We learned not to decide this silently. We ask the customer:

- **Look** means the garment matches the reference picture as closely as possible, even where that fights their proportions.
- **Accuracy** means the garment is drafted proportional to their own measurements, even where that departs from the picture.

Some customers want to step into the screen; others want the garment to flatter them first. Neither answer is wrong, but only the customer can give it, and the answer drives every proportional decision on the draft.

## Correcting a pattern from feedback

After the test garment, customers describe changes in plain words, and our job is to find the pattern change their words point at, not to apply their numbers literally. An example from a screen-inspired tailcoat commission: the customer asked for lapel points that aim "toward the chest rather than the shoulders." On the draft, we took two points on the shoulder line, where the lapel currently pointed and the low shoulder point where the shoulder joins the arm, and redrafted the lapel angle so the new line intersects midway between them. A small geometric change that transformed the coat's character. The same customer asked for "front panels 3.5 inches longer with a more downward angle," which meant two related changes: lengthening the entire front panel by 3.5 inches and redrafting the front cutaway steeper so the points aim toward the toe rather than the mid-thigh.

A correction is never applied to one area in isolation. Every adjustment affects the balance of the entire garment, so the surrounding pattern pieces are reviewed and refined with it. We learned that lesson the way every workshop does: a coat hangs from all of its lines at once.

## Pattern storage and reuse

The corrected pattern is kept on file with the client's measurements. A pattern that has been through a fitting and been signed off is verified knowledge, so repeat orders start from it rather than from zero, adjusted for any change the client reports (see [Measurement](measurement.md)).
