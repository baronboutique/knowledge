The Waistcoat Is the Most Overlooked Piece of Tailoring, and the One That Elevates an Outfit Most
A waistcoat does two jobs. It turns a two-piece suit into a three-piece, adding formality, warmth, and a layered, considered look that photographs well at weddings and formal events. And on its own, over a shirt, it is a sharp, distinctive layer that reads as far more deliberate than a jumper or a cardigan. It is also the piece most often ruined by a poor fit, because a waistcoat that gaps at the buttons, rides up over the trouser waistband, or pulls across the back looks worse than no waistcoat at all.

## Questions & Answers

**Q:** What is the difference between a waistcoat and a vest?  
**A:** They are the same garment: waistcoat is the British term and vest is the American term for the sleeveless, buttoned layer worn over a shirt and under a jacket. Both refer to the tailored front piece with a structured, lined front and often an adjustable strap at the back, as distinct from a knitted sweater vest. Everything in this collection is a tailored waistcoat, made to your measurements.

**Q:** How should a waistcoat fit?  
**A:** A waistcoat should close comfortably at the front without the buttons straining or the fabric gaping between them, and it should be long enough to cover the trouser waistband completely, with no strip of shirt showing between the bottom of the waistcoat and the top of the trousers. The back should sit flat, adjusted with the rear strap for a clean line. Because fit at the front and length are the two things a waistcoat gets wrong most often, ours is made to your measurements and confirmed on a free test garment before the cloth is cut.

**Q:** Should the bottom button of a waistcoat be left undone?  
**A:** By tradition, yes, the bottom button of a waistcoat is left open. The convention dates back to waistcoats cut with the bottom button positioned so it naturally sits open for ease of movement, and it has become the accepted way to wear one. Leaving it undone also lets the waistcoat sit more comfortably when you sit down. It is a small detail, but it is one of the quiet signals that someone knows how tailoring is worn.

**Q:** Can I add a matching waistcoat to a suit to make it a three-piece?  
**A:** Yes. A waistcoat is the standard way to turn a two-piece suit into a three-piece, and because it is made to your measurements in the same cloth, it matches the suit exactly rather than being a close approximation. Order the waistcoat alongside a suit from the Suits &amp; Blazers collection, or add one to a wedding suit. A three-piece adds formality and warmth and gives you the option of removing the jacket while still looking dressed.

**Q:** Worsted wool or linen, which should I choose?  
**A:** Choose worsted wool for a year-round, formal waistcoat that works as the third piece of a suit and holds a crisp line in any season. Choose linen for summer weddings, garden parties, and hot climates, where its breathability keeps you cool and its relaxed texture suits a lighter, warm-weather look. If you want a waistcoat mainly for formal three-piece wear, wool is the safer default; if it is for a summer event, linen is the better fit.

**Q:** Can I wear a waistcoat without a jacket?  
**A:** Yes, and it is one of the most useful ways to wear one. A well-fitted waistcoat over a dress shirt, with or without a tie, reads as intentional and sharp, and at a wedding or event it lets the groom or guests remove their jackets at the reception while still looking tailored. Because the waistcoat is made to your measurements, it holds its shape and line without a jacket over it, which is exactly where a poorly fitted, off-the-rack waistcoat falls apart.

**Q:** Are the waistcoats made to my measurements?  
**A:** Yes. Both waistcoats are made to your measurements with no standard sizes. You enter your measurements on the product page, and before we cut your final cloth we send a free test garment so the fit is confirmed on your body first. This matters more on a waistcoat than most people expect, because the close fit at the front and the exact length over the waistband are what separate a sharp waistcoat from a poor one.

**Q:** How do I care for the waistcoat?  
**A:** Both the worsted wool and linen waistcoats are best dry cleaned to preserve the structure of the lined front and the shape of the cloth. Between wears, hang the waistcoat on a hanger rather than folding it, so the front keeps its line. Linen will relax and soften with wear, which is part of its character; a light press restores a crisp look for an event.

If your date is sooner, contact us before ordering and we will confirm whether the timeline works.
