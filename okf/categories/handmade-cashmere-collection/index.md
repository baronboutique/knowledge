Most “Cashmere” Is Blended, Machine-Made, or Mislabeled. Ours Is None of Those.
The word cashmere is one of the most abused labels in fashion. A great deal of what is sold as cashmere is blended with wool, viscose, or acrylic, spun and woven by machine, and finished to feel soft in the shop for exactly as long as it takes to get it home. Within a season it pills, thins, and loses the warmth that made it worth buying. Genuine cashmere behaves the opposite way: it gets softer with every wash, holds its warmth for decades, and reads as quiet luxury rather than costume.

## Questions & Answers

**Q:** What is Grade A cashmere, and why does it matter?  
**A:** Cashmere is graded on the length and fineness of the fiber. Grade A is the longest and finest, typically measuring 14 to 16 microns in diameter with a long staple, which is what gives it superior warmth without bulk, a soft handle that improves rather than degrades with wear, and strong resistance to pilling. Grade B and C fibers are shorter and coarser, which is why cheaper cashmere feels acceptable at first but pills and thins within a season. Every piece in this collection is 100% Grade A cashmere with no blend, which is the single biggest reason it behaves the way genuine cashmere should.

**Q:** Is pashmina the same as cashmere?  
**A:** Pashmina is a type of cashmere, specifically the very fine undercoat of the Changthangi goat of the Himalayan highlands, measuring around 12 to 16 microns, which is finer than most standard cashmere. So all pashmina is cashmere, but not all cashmere is pashmina. Our pashmina shawl is 100% Grade A Himalayan pashmina at a 200/2 yarn count, handwoven over five days, while the rest of the collection is 100% Grade A cashmere. Both are genuine single-fiber pieces, never blended with wool, viscose, or synthetic.

**Q:** How do I tell real cashmere from fake?  
**A:** Genuine cashmere passes tests that blends fail. The feel test: real cashmere is warm to the touch before you put it on, because the fiber traps air; synthetic blends feel cool and slightly slick. The pill test: quality Grade A cashmere with a long staple resists pilling, while short-fiber or blended cashmere pills heavily after a wash or two. The burn test: a fringe thread of real cashmere smells like burnt hair and crumbles to ash, whereas synthetic content melts and smells of plastic. The weave test: handwoven cashmere shows slight irregularities against the light, while a perfectly uniform weave signals machine production. Every piece here is 100% Grade A, hand-spun and handwoven.

**Q:** Does cashmere pill or shed?  
**A:** High-quality cashmere made from long Grade A fibers should not shed and pills only minimally, and any slight pilling in the first weeks is loose surface fiber that stops once it works itself out; a gentle cashmere comb removes it. Heavy, persistent pilling and shedding are signs of short-fiber or blended cashmere, not of cashmere itself. Because this collection uses long-staple Grade A fiber, the pieces stay smooth and actually grow softer with careful washing over the years.

**Q:** How do I wash and care for cashmere?  
**A:** Hand washing is best. Use cool to lukewarm water with a wool-and-cashmere shampoo, never hot water or regular detergent, both of which damage the fiber. Submerge and gently agitate, soak for up to 30 minutes, rinse in cool water, then squeeze gently without wringing and lay flat on a towel to dry in the shade, away from direct heat and sun. Steam or iron on the lowest setting with a pressing cloth if needed. Stored folded in a breathable bag with cedar or lavender to deter moths, a cashmere piece lasts for decades. Gentle dry cleaning is also fine.

**Q:** What is the difference between a cashmere scarf, wrap, and shawl?  
**A:** The difference is size. A scarf is narrower, roughly 12 to 22 inches wide, made to loop or knot around the neck. A wrap is wider and works both around the neck and across the shoulders. A shawl is the widest, around 36 inches, made to drape fully across the shoulders or the body, which is why the pashmina shawl at 36 by 80 inches doubles as a travel blanket. This collection includes all three, so the right choice depends on whether you want a neck accessory, a versatile in-between, or a full shoulder drape.

**Q:** How long does a cashmere scarf or shawl last?  
**A:** A 100% Grade A cashmere piece, washed and stored correctly, lasts 20 to 30 years and improves over that time rather than wearing out. Because cashmere is a protein fiber similar to human hair, it softens with washing instead of weakening, and it develops a depth of drape that new cashmere does not have. The pieces that fail within a few seasons are almost always blends. The limiting factor on genuine cashmere is not the fiber, it is whether the gentle care instructions are followed.
